# Patient Rights and Laws: Reference for the Negotiation Agent

> **Disclaimer:** This is general information, not legal advice. Laws, thresholds, and court rulings change — verify any fact before citing it in a formal dispute or appeal. Last verified: July 2026.

This file is written for an agent acting on a patient's behalf. Each section explains the law, then gives concrete leverage moves and exact phrases the patient (or the agent drafting for them) can use.

---

## 1. No Surprises Act (effective January 1, 2022)

**What it protects against.** The No Surprises Act (NSA) bans most "surprise" balance bills for insured patients:

- **Emergency care:** Out-of-network (OON) emergency services — including the ER visit and post-stabilization care — must be billed at in-network cost-sharing rates. The patient owes only what they would have owed in-network; the plan and provider fight over the rest through federal arbitration (IDR), not through the patient.
- **Non-emergency care at in-network facilities:** If the patient goes to an in-network hospital or surgery center but is treated by an OON provider (anesthesiologist, radiologist, pathologist, assistant surgeon, hospitalist, lab), the patient can only be charged in-network cost-sharing. Providers of ancillary services (anesthesiology, pathology, radiology, neonatology, diagnostics) can **never** ask the patient to waive this protection. Other OON providers can only balance-bill if the patient signed a specific written notice-and-consent form at least 72 hours in advance — and even then only in limited circumstances.
- **Air ambulances** are covered. **Ground ambulances are NOT** — that remains a major gap (some states fill it).

**Good Faith Estimates (GFE).** Uninsured and self-pay patients have the right to a written Good Faith Estimate of expected charges before scheduled care (and on request). Providers must give it within 1–3 business days of scheduling, depending on lead time. (GFE/Advanced-EOB provisions for *insured* patients exist in the statute but enforcement is still deferred pending rulemaking — as of mid-2026, GFE rights are practically for uninsured/self-pay patients.)

**Patient-Provider Dispute Resolution (PPDR).** If an uninsured/self-pay patient got a GFE and the final bill from that provider is **at least $400 more** than the GFE for that provider:

- File with CMS within **120 calendar days** of receiving the initial bill.
- The fee is **$25** (recouped from the provider if the patient wins).
- File online at https://www.cms.gov/medical-bill-rights (the portal walks through it), or by mail/fax.
- An independent dispute resolution entity decides what the patient owes; if the patient wins, they generally pay the GFE amount. The provider **cannot send the bill to collections or charge late fees while the dispute is pending**.

**Complaints.** For any suspected NSA violation (balance bill for emergency care, no GFE offered, OON billing at an in-network facility): file at https://www.cms.gov/medical-bill-rights (formerly cms.gov/nosurprises) or call the No Surprises Help Desk at **1-800-985-3059**. Complaints are free and CMS investigates.

**How the agent uses this:**
- First triage every bill: emergency care? In-network facility with OON provider? If yes, the balance bill is likely illegal — don't negotiate it, dispute it.
- Exact phrase: *"This appears to be a balance bill prohibited by the No Surprises Act. I was treated at an in-network facility / received emergency care, and federal law limits my cost-sharing to in-network amounts. Please rebill correctly or I will file a complaint with CMS at 1-800-985-3059."*
- For self-pay patients: always request a GFE before scheduled care, in writing, and keep it. It is the anchor for any later PPDR dispute.
- If the bill exceeds the GFE by $400+: *"This bill exceeds my Good Faith Estimate by more than $400. I intend to initiate the federal Patient-Provider Dispute Resolution process within the 120-day window unless this is corrected."* Providers often adjust to the GFE rather than face PPDR.
- Never let a patient sign an OON consent form for ancillary services (anesthesia, radiology, pathology) — those waivers are invalid by law.
- Check the deadline math immediately: PPDR is 120 days from the first bill. If close, file first, negotiate second.

## 2. IRS 501(r): Charity Care at Nonprofit Hospitals

Roughly half of US hospitals are 501(c)(3) nonprofits. Under IRC §501(r) (ACA, 2010; final regs 2014), keeping tax-exempt status requires:

- **Financial Assistance Policy (FAP):** Every nonprofit hospital must have a written FAP stating eligibility criteria (usually income as % of Federal Poverty Level), the discounts/free care available, and how to apply. The FAP, application form, and a plain-language summary must be **freely and publicly available** — on the website, on request, in the ER and admissions areas, and in billing statements.
- **AGB limit:** FAP-eligible patients cannot be charged more than "**amounts generally billed**" (AGB) to insured patients — i.e., roughly what Medicare or private insurers actually pay, not chargemaster rates. Gross charges to FAP-eligible patients are prohibited.
- **240-day application period:** Hospitals must accept FAP applications for **at least 240 days from the first post-discharge billing statement**. A bill already in collections can still be within the application window.
- **No extraordinary collection actions (ECAs) before eligibility check:** The hospital must make "reasonable efforts" to determine FAP eligibility before any ECA, and must wait **at least 120 days from the first post-discharge bill** before starting ECAs. ECAs include: reporting to credit bureaus, selling the debt, lawsuits, liens, wage garnishment, and denying/deferring care because of an unpaid prior bill. If the patient applies during the 240 days, ECAs must be suspended and reversed if eligibility is found.

**Check nonprofit status:**
- IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search: https://apps.irs.gov/app/eos/ (successor to Pub 78 data)
- ProPublica Nonprofit Explorer: https://projects.propublica.org/nonprofits/ (also shows the hospital's Form 990 Schedule H — charity care spending — useful shaming leverage)

**States with stronger charity-care laws** (state law stacks on top of 501(r) and often covers for-profit hospitals too — always check current thresholds):
- **Washington:** All hospitals must give free or discounted care on a sliding scale; large systems must give free care to low-income patients well above the poverty line — among the most generous in the country.
- **California:** Hospital Fair Pricing Act — uninsured/high-medical-cost patients up to 400% FPL get charity care or discounted rates capped near Medicare levels; applies to for-profits too.
- **New York:** Hospital Financial Assistance Law — sliding-scale discounts mandatory at all hospitals; expanded in 2024 with higher income limits and collection restrictions.
- **Colorado:** Hospital Discounted Care — hospitals must screen patients for eligibility (≤250% FPL) before collections, cap payments, and offer payment plans; screening failure = penalties.
- **New Jersey:** Charity Care Program — free care for low incomes, sliding scale above, at all acute-care hospitals.
- **Illinois:** Hospital Uninsured Patient Discount Act — mandatory discounts for uninsured patients and an annual out-of-pocket cap tied to income.
- **Oregon:** Nonprofit hospitals must provide free care to lower-income patients and steep sliding-scale discounts well above that.
- **Maine:** Hospitals must provide free care to patients below statutory income limits.
- **New Mexico:** Patients' Debt Collection Protection Act — bans collection actions entirely against patients below statutory income limits and restricts reporting.

**How the agent uses this:**
- Step one on ANY hospital bill: check nonprofit status via ProPublica, then find the FAP (search "<hospital name> financial assistance policy" — it must be posted).
- Exact phrase: *"I'm requesting a copy of your Financial Assistance Policy, application form, and plain-language summary, as required under IRS Section 501(r)."*
- Screen the patient against FAP income criteria BEFORE negotiating. A bill that qualifies for 100% charity care should never be settled for cash.
- The 240-day window is a reset button: a bill in collections that's under ~8 months old can still be wiped by a FAP application. Exact phrase: *"I am submitting a financial assistance application within the 240-day application period required by 501(r). Please suspend all collection activity pending an eligibility determination."*
- If the hospital reported to a credit bureau or sued within 120 days of the first bill, or without FAP screening: *"This appears to be an extraordinary collection action taken without the reasonable efforts to determine FAP eligibility that 501(r)(6) requires. Please reverse it, or I will report this to the IRS (Form 13909) and my state attorney general."*
- Even for patients above FAP thresholds, AGB is an anchor: nonprofit hospitals demonstrably accept Medicare-ish rates. Offer accordingly.
- In the strong-law states above, cite the state statute too — state AGs enforce these and hospitals know it.

## 3. Hospital Price Transparency Rule (45 CFR §180, effective 2021)

Every US hospital must publish:

1. A **machine-readable file (MRF)** of standard charges for all items and services: gross (chargemaster) charge, discounted cash price, payer-specific negotiated rates, and de-identified min/max negotiated charges. Since mid-2024 this must be in a CMS-standardized format (CSV/JSON), and the CY2026 OPPS rule (enforcement from **April 1, 2026**) requires actual dollar amounts — median plus 10th/90th percentile allowed amounts computed from real remittance data — plus a stronger accuracy attestation.
2. A **consumer-friendly display** of shoppable services (or a price-estimator tool).

CMS audits hospitals and imposes civil monetary penalties for noncompliance; you can complain to CMS if a hospital's file is missing or junk.

**How the agent uses this:**
- Find the MRF: search "<hospital name> machine readable standard charges" or check the hospital website footer; aggregators like turquoise.health and dolthub also mirror many files.
- Pull three numbers for each billed CPT/DRG code: the **cash price**, the **median/lowest negotiated rate**, and the Medicare rate. Bills are usually chargemaster rates, often 3–10x what insurers actually pay.
- Exact phrase: *"Your own published machine-readable file shows a discounted cash price of $X and negotiated rates as low as $Y for CPT code Z. I'm prepared to pay [cash price or median negotiated rate]. Billing me several times what every insurer pays is not a defensible price."*
- If the hospital has no MRF or it's unusable, that's leverage too: *"I couldn't locate the standard-charges file required by 45 CFR Part 180. I'll be filing a price transparency complaint with CMS unless we can resolve this bill."*
- Cross-check with fairhealthconsumer.org and the Medicare fee schedule to triangulate a fair price before making an offer.

## 4. FDCPA and Medical Debt in Collections

The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (15 U.S.C. §1692 et seq.) applies to third-party collectors (and debt buyers), not usually the hospital's own billing office. Key rights:

- **Debt validation (§1692g):** Within 5 days of first contact, the collector must send a validation notice (Regulation F standardized it). The patient has **30 days from receiving that notice** to dispute the debt in writing; the collector must then stop collecting until it mails verification. Disputing also flags the debt as disputed on any credit report.
- **Cease communication (§1692c(c)):** A written demand stops all collector contact except confirming they'll stop or noting a specific legal action.
- **No false/deceptive practices (§1692e):** Includes threatening lawsuits they can't/won't file, misrepresenting the amount, or implying credit reporting that isn't permitted.
- **Statute of limitations:** Each state sets a limitations period for suing on a debt (commonly 3–6 years; varies widely). Once expired, the debt is "time-barred" — still owed in theory, but unenforceable in court. Under Regulation F, suing or threatening to sue on time-barred debt is a violation.
- **Re-aging trap:** In many states, a partial payment or written acknowledgment of an old debt **restarts the statute of limitations**. Never advise paying "a little to show good faith" on old debt before checking the state's rule and the debt's age.

**How the agent uses this:**
- On first collector contact, ALWAYS send a written validation request within the 30-day window — certified mail or the collector's portal, keep proof. Exact phrase: *"I dispute this debt and request validation under 15 U.S.C. §1692g, including an itemized statement of the original charges, the original creditor, and documentation that you are entitled to collect."*
- Medical debts frequently fail validation (wrong amounts, missing itemization, insurance never billed correctly). No validation = no collection.
- Before any settlement talk on old debt: determine (a) date of last payment/activity, (b) the state's statute of limitations. If time-barred, negotiating posture changes completely — they cannot sue. But warn the patient: paying or acknowledging may revive it.
- Route disputes back upstream: if the debt is 501(r)-eligible (Section 2) or an NSA violation (Section 1), tell the collector the underlying debt is invalid and simultaneously work the hospital.
- Violations (harassment, false threats, collecting disputed debt without verification) → complaint to CFPB (consumerfinance.gov/complaint) and state AG; violations also create settlement leverage.

## 5. Medical Debt and Credit Reports (status as of July 2026 — VOLATILE, re-verify)

**The CFPB rule is dead.** The CFPB finalized a rule in January 2025 (amending Regulation V) that would have removed all medical debt from credit reports and barred lenders from using it. It never took effect: on **July 11, 2025**, the US District Court for the Eastern District of Texas (*Cornerstone Credit Union League v. CFPB*) **vacated the rule nationwide** — with the CFPB itself (under new leadership) joining the plaintiffs in asking for vacatur, agreeing it exceeded the Bureau's FCRA authority. Congressional Review Act resolutions against the rule were mooted. No successful appeal has revived it as of mid-2026.

**What still protects patients — voluntary bureau policies (in effect, but not law):** Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion voluntarily adopted, and still maintain:
- **Paid** medical collections are removed from credit reports entirely.
- Medical collections **under $500** are not reported at all.
- Unpaid medical collections can't appear until **at least 1 year** past due (vs. 6 months before).
- Also: medical debt is excluded from newer scoring models (VantageScore 3.0/4.0 ignore it; FICO 9/10 weight it less), though many lenders still use older FICO models.

**State laws:** As of early 2026, **15 states** have enacted their own restrictions on medical debt credit reporting: **California, Colorado, Connecticut, Delaware, Illinois, Maine, Maryland, Minnesota, New Jersey, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Vermont, Virginia, and Washington** (details vary — some ban CRAs from including it, some ban furnishing it, some ban creditors from using it; several took effect during 2025–Jan 2026). **Caveat:** the Texas court's opinion included non-binding dicta suggesting FCRA may preempt such state laws; preemption challenges are a live litigation risk, but the state laws remain on the books and enforceable unless struck down. Re-verify the patient's state before relying on this.

**How the agent uses this:**
- Get the patient's reports free at annualcreditreport.com (now free weekly) and inventory every medical item.
- Any **paid** medical collection or one **under $500** on a report violates the bureaus' own policies: dispute it with each bureau. Exact phrase: *"This is a medical collection [that has been paid / under $500 / less than one year old], which under the nationwide credit reporting agencies' current medical debt policies should not appear on my report. Please delete it."*
- If the patient is in one of the 15 ban states, cite the state law in the dispute and complain to the state AG/financial regulator if the furnisher won't budge.
- Deletion is negotiation currency: when settling a reported medical collection, get **written agreement** that the account will be deleted upon payment (with the bureau policy, deletion of paid medical debt should happen anyway — but get it in writing).
- Do NOT tell a patient "medical debt can't hurt your credit anymore" — the federal rule was vacated. The accurate frame: under-$500, paid, and under-1-year debts stay off; larger unpaid debts CAN still be reported (outside ban states).
- Warn patients that mortgage lending still often uses older FICO models where a medical collection stings — another reason to resolve before it's reported (see the 501(r) 120-day ECA clock, Section 2).

## 6. HIPAA Right of Access (45 CFR §164.524)

Patients have a federal right to obtain their own medical records — including the **designated record set**: medical records AND **billing records** (itemized bills, claims submitted to insurance, payment records).

- **Timeline:** The provider must respond within **30 calendar days**, with one 30-day extension allowed only if they give written notice with a reason within the first 30 days.
- **Cost:** Only a **reasonable, cost-based fee** — labor to copy, supplies, postage. NOT retrieval fees, review fees, or per-page state-law rates when the patient requests electronically. Electronic copies are often effectively free or a small flat fee.
- **Format:** Patient can request electronic copies and direct records to a third party (like the agent's workspace).
- **Enforcement:** HHS Office for Civil Rights actively enforces right-of-access violations (an ongoing enforcement initiative with dozens of settlements). Complaints: https://www.hhs.gov/ocr/complaints/

**How the agent uses this:**
- The itemized bill and records are the raw material of every negotiation — request them first, always. Exact phrase: *"Under my HIPAA right of access (45 CFR 164.524), I request an itemized bill with CPT/HCPCS and revenue codes, plus the medical records for these dates of service, in electronic format. You have 30 days to respond, and may charge only a reasonable, cost-based fee."*
- Asking for codes matters: a "summary bill" hides upcoding, duplicate charges, and services never rendered. Cross-check itemized codes against the medical record (was that Level 5 ER visit documented? Was that supply actually used?).
- If stonewalled past 30 days or quoted inflated "records fees": *"HIPAA permits only reasonable, cost-based fees for access requests, and requires a response within 30 days. If I don't receive the records, I'll file a complaint with the HHS Office for Civil Rights, which is actively enforcing right-of-access violations."*
- A provider that won't produce an itemized bill is also a provider that can't validate the debt (Section 4) — press both levers.

## 7. Insurance Denials: ACA and ERISA Appeal Rights (brief)

Most denials are appealable, and appeals succeed far more often than people expect — but deadlines are strict:

- **Internal appeal:** Patient has **180 days** from the denial notice to file with the plan. Plan must decide within **30 days** (service not yet received) or **60 days** (service already received); **72 hours** for urgent care appeals.
- **External review:** After a final internal denial (or simultaneously if urgent), the patient can request independent external review — federal baseline deadline is **4 months** after the final adverse determination (some states differ). External reviewer decides within **45–60 days** (72 hours expedited), and the decision **binds the insurer**.
- **ERISA plans** (most employer coverage) follow similar internal-appeal rules; exhausting internal appeals preserves the right to sue in federal court. Self-funded employer plans use the federal external review process, not state processes.
- The denial letter must state the reason, the specific plan provision, and appeal instructions. The patient is entitled — free — to the plan's internal documents relied on for the denial (claim file, clinical criteria).

**How the agent uses this:**
- Never negotiate a bill that exists only because insurance wrongly denied it — appeal the denial first. Calendar the 180-day and 4-month deadlines immediately.
- Exact phrase for the claim file: *"I request, free of charge, copies of all documents, records, and clinical criteria relied upon for this adverse benefit determination, as required for a full and fair review."*
- Common winnable denials: "not medically necessary" (rebut with treating-physician letter + clinical guidelines), coding errors (fix and resubmit), "out-of-network" for care covered by the No Surprises Act (Section 1 — cite it in the appeal).
- Always escalate to external review after an internal loss — it's free, independent, and binding.
- State insurance departments and (for ERISA plans) the US Department of Labor (1-866-444-3272) take complaints and often shake claims loose.

## 8. Taxes on Forgiven Medical Debt (1099-C)

When a creditor cancels a debt of **$600 or more**, the patient may receive
a **Form 1099-C (Cancellation of Debt)**, and the forgiven amount is treated
as taxable income by default — a $14,000 forgiveness at a 22% marginal rate
would mean ~$3,080 of tax on money never received. Key qualifiers:

- **Not every write-off is income.** Corrected billing errors and disputed
  amounts are price adjustments, not debt cancellation — not taxable. The
  same goes for charity-care adjustments under a Financial Assistance
  Policy, which the IRS treats as the hospital's price reduction, not
  forgiveness of an enforceable debt. Hospitals also generally are not
  "applicable entities" required to file 1099-Cs, so most negotiated
  reductions never generate one. Third-party debt buyers settling for less,
  however, sometimes do.
- **The insolvency exclusion.** If total liabilities exceeded total assets
  at the moment of forgiveness — true for many people carrying large
  medical debt — some or all of the canceled amount can be excluded from
  income by filing **IRS Form 982** with that year's return. Document
  assets and liabilities as of the forgiveness date.
- This is squarely tax-professional territory: flag it, prepare the
  numbers, and tell the user to confirm with a preparer — do not compute
  their return.

**How the agent uses this:**
- When a settlement or forgiveness lands, warn the user a 1099-C *may*
  arrive the following January and record the forgiveness date and amount
  in the case file (that's the insolvency snapshot date).
- If a 1099-C arrives for a *disputed or error-corrected* amount, the fix
  is upstream: ask the issuer to correct/withdraw the 1099-C, in writing.
- Frame settlements as "corrected balance" / "billing adjustment" rather
  than "forgiveness" where truthful — the wording in the settlement letter
  matters later.
