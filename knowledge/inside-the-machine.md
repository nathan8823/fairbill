# Inside the machine: how the bill actually gets made

Negotiation works because a hospital bill is not a fact — it is the output
of a chain of discretionary, revenue-optimizing decisions made without the
patient in the room. Knowing the chain tells you where the leverage is.
(These are documented industry practices; the framing below is synthesized
from billing-insider accounts and revenue-cycle industry sources.)

## The five decisions made without the patient

1. **Pre-admission screening.** Before a scheduled patient arrives, revenue
   cycle systems commonly pull demographic and credit-derived data and
   assign a **propensity-to-pay score**. That score influences how the
   account is worked — payment-plan offers, financial-assistance
   pre-screening, collections routing. *Leverage: the hospital modeled
   whether you'd fight; documentation and persistence break the model's
   assumptions.*
2. **Coding.** After discharge, a coder the patient never meets translates
   the chart into CPT/HCPCS and DRG codes. Coding choices (visit level,
   complications, bundling) directly set the price. *Leverage: the codes
   are checkable against the medical record — that's the `review-bill`
   audit.*
3. **Claims adjudication.** The insurer's utilization-review process
   decides what's covered, what's medically necessary, and what's denied.
   *Leverage: appeals exist precisely because this is discretionary —
   `insurance-appeal`.*
4. **Denial management.** The hospital decides which insurer denials to
   fight and which to write off — and written-off denials often just become
   the patient's balance. *Leverage: ask, in writing, whether a denied
   claim was appealed by the provider before accepting any balance that
   exists because of a denial the provider chose not to fight — especially
   when the denial was caused by the provider's own coding or
   authorization error.*
5. **Collections scoring.** Before referral to collections, accounts are
   scored again to decide how aggressively to pursue. *Leverage: disputed,
   documented accounts are expensive to pursue; clean documented disputes
   get settled, not sued.*

## The timing trap: the bill beats the EOB

Hospital billing systems typically drop the patient statement immediately,
while coding and claim submission to insurance take days or weeks. Result:
**patients receive a "full amount" bill before insurance has processed —
or even received — the claim.** Many people pay it, or start payment plans,
before their insurer has reduced the balance to what they actually owe.

Rules this implies (already in AGENTS.md, now with the why):

- **Insured: never pay before the EOB exists and matches the bill.** The
  EOB is the only document that shows what is actually owed.
- A bill that arrives suspiciously fast after service is a flag to ask:
  *"Has this claim been submitted to my insurance? On what date?"*
- If a claim was never filed or was filed wrong (wrong payer, coding
  error), the fix is resubmission by the provider — not payment by the
  patient. Most plans give providers a timely-filing window; a provider
  who blew it generally must write the balance off, not bill the patient.

## What actually scares billing departments

From a retired hospital CFO's summary, and consistent with everything in
this playbook: not lawsuit threats, not angry calls, not even regulator
complaints — **documentation**. Patients who put every request in writing,
send certified letters instead of making phone calls, and keep every bill,
EOB, record, and response. Documented accounts are expensive: every
discretionary decision in the chain above now has to survive scrutiny, and
the collections-profit math stops working. One judge's summary of civil
disputes applies: the side with the most paper generally wins.

Two structural notes that follow:

- **The compliance office outranks billing.** Hospital compliance
  departments answer for regulatory exposure (501(r), EMTALA, transparency,
  coding integrity). CC'ing the compliance office on a documented dispute
  letter — factually, not threateningly — puts the account in front of
  people with authority and different incentives than the billing queue.
  Use at escalation rung 4+, not as an opener.
- **Patient statements are generated by volume, disputed by exception.**
  The system assumes non-response. A written dispute takes an account out
  of the automated flow and forces a human decision — which is exactly
  where negotiation begins.
