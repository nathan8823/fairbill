# Before care: preventing the bad bill

The cheapest bill to fight is the one that never gets generated. Whenever the
user mentions *upcoming* care — a scheduled procedure, a referral, a new
provider — run this playbook first. It takes minutes and routinely prevents
four-figure surprises.

## The four questions (ask at every encounter)

Give these to the user verbatim, and log the answers (who said it, date) in
a note they keep:

1. **At scheduling:** *"Is this provider in-network with my insurance for
   this specific type of visit?"* — Not just "in-network." Specialty visits,
   procedures, and diagnostic tests can carry different network rules than
   primary care. Get the visit type stated.
2. **At check-in:** *"Do you have a financial assistance program, and can I
   have the application?"* — Every time, regardless of expected cost.
   Having the application in hand costs nothing; needing it later without
   it has cost patients thousands (and having asked *at intake* is
   documented evidence the hospital knew to screen — useful under 501(r)).
3. **Before any test or referral:** *"Where will this be processed, and is
   that facility in-network?"* — Labs, imaging, and specialists ordered by
   an in-network doctor are each a separate network decision and a separate
   potential bill. The referring office can usually redirect to an
   in-network lab if asked — but only if asked before.
4. **After any bill arrives:** *"Is this your best price if I pay today?"*
   — Every time, without exception. Sometimes the answer is a 20–40%
   prompt-pay discount that exists only if the words are said. If the rep
   says no discount exists, ask for a financial counselor or supervisor —
   front-line reps often can't see discount programs that counselors can
   apply. (Don't actually pay on the spot for anything large — get the
   quoted price in writing and run the normal verification first.)

## Good Faith Estimates (self-pay/uninsured)

For scheduled care, request the GFE in writing and keep it — it's the anchor
for the federal $400-overage dispute process later. Details:
`rights-and-laws.md` §1, `benchmarks.md` §5.

## The admissions agreement — what's actually being signed

Standard hospital admission paperwork typically contains:

- **Assignment of benefits** — authorizes the hospital to receive and
  negotiate insurance payment directly. Standard, but it means the claim is
  worked without the patient in the loop — one more reason to demand the
  EOB and never pay before it exists.
- **Financial responsibility** — an open-ended agreement to pay "all
  charges not covered by insurance," signed before any charge exists. This
  is why billers say "you agreed to pay." The counter (see
  `objections.md`): a blanket form doesn't waive error disputes, No
  Surprises Act protections, or FAP eligibility.
- **Consent to treatment** — often broad enough to authorize treatment by
  any member of the care team, including providers the patient never meets
  who bill separately. This is where surprise pathologist/radiologist/
  anesthesiologist bills come from; the No Surprises Act now blocks most of
  them at in-network facilities (`rights-and-laws.md` §1).
- **Arbitration clause** (some hospitals) — waives the right to sue in
  favor of private arbitration. On paper forms this can often be struck
  through before signing — initial the strike-through and keep a copy.
  Honestly: on electronic signature pads there is usually no way to modify
  the text; the realistic options are asking for a paper copy, writing a
  contemporaneous note documenting objection, or simply knowing what was
  signed.

Practical rule for the agent: the user should never feel pressure to
lawyer-up at check-in — but a photo of every page they sign, taken on their
phone, costs nothing and turns "you agreed to X" into a checkable claim.

## Never sign away No Surprises Act protections

Out-of-network providers may present a notice-and-consent form that waives
balance-billing protection. For **ancillary services** (anesthesiology,
pathology, radiology, labs, neonatology, assistant surgeons, hospitalists),
such waivers are **invalid by law** — decline to sign, and note that
emergency care can never require them. For other providers, signing is
almost never in the patient's interest; the in-network alternative is the
ask.
